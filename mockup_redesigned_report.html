Section 7 · Should you walk?

Walk away if any of these happen

We're not telling you to walk. We're telling you the conditions where walking is the right move. Most buyers fall in love with the house and overpay because no one gave them permission to leave.

  • If the seller refuses repair credit on the sewer lateral. A sewer line failure costs $15–30K to remediate and you'll be living through it. This is a hard line.
  • If the seller refuses to extend inspection contingency. The foundation crack is structural — you need a $400 specialist opinion before you can responsibly close.
  • If their counter is above $2,650,000. The comp data doesn't support paying more. At that point you're funding their renovation, not buying a house.
  • If three or more disclosure omissions stay uncorrected after a written response. That's a pattern, not an oversight. Pattern means the seller will keep finding ways to surprise you between now and close.

Walking is a real option. The Bay Area has inventory. This is not the only house.

Methodology

This analysis cross-references the seller's TDS (Transfer Disclosure Statement) against the inspection report's findings, weights each cross-reference by inspector-documented severity and our recall classifier's confidence, and computes the recommended offer as: asking — confirmed repair cost — hidden-issue reserve (≥70% confidence predictions only) — disclosure-omission penalty — market-discount-vs-comps. Walk-away is recommended offer −10%; aggressive opening is recommended offer −6%.
