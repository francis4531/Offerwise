California Home Inspection: The Complete Buyer's Guide

California doesn't legally require a home inspection — but skipping one is the most expensive mistake a buyer can make. Here's what they cost, what they should cover, and how the contingency works.

📅 Updated May 2026⏱️ 12 min read

One of the most common questions first-time California homebuyers ask: is a home inspection required? The short answer is no — California law does not mandate that buyers get a home inspection before purchasing a property. But the longer answer is more nuanced.

What California Law Actually Requires

While buyers aren't required to get an inspection, California law does require several things that make inspections practically essential:

  • Seller disclosure is mandatory. Under Civil Code §1102, sellers must provide a Transfer Disclosure Statement (TDS) disclosing known defects. But sellers only disclose what they know — or claim to know.
  • Agent inspection is mandatory. Under Civil Code §2079, real estate agents must conduct a reasonably competent visual inspection of accessible areas. But this is limited to what's visible — no crawlspaces, no roofs, no attics.
  • The buyer has the right to inspect. Standard California purchase contracts include an inspection contingency giving buyers the right to conduct inspections at their own expense during the contingency period.

The gap between what the seller discloses and what an agent can see is enormous. That gap is exactly what a professional home inspection fills.

What California Home Inspectors Must Cover

Home inspections in California are governed by the Business and Professions Code §7195-7199. California inspectors must follow Standards of Practice set by their certifying organization (typically InterNACHI or ASHI). A standard inspection must include:

  • Structural components (foundation, framing, floors, walls, ceilings, roof structure)
  • Exterior surfaces and components (siding, trim, drainage)
  • Roofing (covering materials, drainage, flashing, skylights)
  • Plumbing (supply lines, drain lines, water heater, fixtures)
  • Electrical (service entrance, panels, branch circuits, outlets)
  • Heating and cooling systems
  • Interior components (walls, ceilings, floors, doors, windows, stairs)
  • Insulation and ventilation
  • Fireplaces and chimneys

Important limitation: California law specifies that home inspections are "noninvasive." Inspectors cannot cut into walls, dig up foundations, or disassemble systems. They report on what they can see and access. This is why certain issues — hidden mold, sewer line condition, geological problems — may require separate specialist inspections.

How Much Does a California Home Inspection Cost?

A standard California home inspection costs $400 to $700 for an average single-family home. Bay Area and Los Angeles County buyers typically pay more — expect $500 to $900 for homes in San Francisco, Oakland, San Jose, and most of LA County. Inland California (Sacramento, Bakersfield, Fresno) tends to run $300 to $550.

The buyer pays. This is true in nearly every California transaction, and it is paid directly to the inspector at or before the inspection — not bundled into closing costs.

What Drives the Price

Three factors move the number most:

  • Square footage. Most inspectors price by home size. A 1,200 sq ft condo runs lower; a 3,500 sq ft single-family home runs higher.
  • Age of the home. Pre-1978 homes (which make up a huge share of California's housing stock) often add $50-$150 because there is more to look at — older electrical, original plumbing, lead paint risk.
  • Property complexity. Multi-unit, hillside, or properties with detached structures (ADUs, guest houses) cost more because there is literally more square footage and more systems to evaluate.

A buyer doing thorough due diligence in California should budget $700 to $1,500 total for inspections on an average single-family home — counting the base inspection plus pest, sewer scope, and any specialist follow-ups. On a million-dollar purchase, that is well under 0.2% of the transaction price, and it is the single best money you will spend protecting that investment.

California Home Inspection Checklist: What Actually Matters

A standard inspection covers the same major systems anywhere in the country: roof, electrical, plumbing, HVAC, foundation, attic, and interior. But California buyers face a specific set of issues that drive most negotiation disputes after inspection. Watch closely for these.

Seismic and Structural

California sits on multiple active fault systems. Most homes built before 1980 — and many between 1980 and 2000 — were not built to current seismic standards. Look for:

  • Foundation type. Raised foundations (common in homes built before 1960) are more vulnerable. Slab foundations are safer but harder to repair if damaged.
  • Seismic retrofit history. Has the home been bolted to its foundation? Are cripple walls braced? Ask for permit records. Unretrofitted homes can lose insurability or get charged 2-3x normal earthquake premiums.
  • Cracks in the foundation. Hairline cracks are often cosmetic. Stair-step cracks, horizontal cracks, or any crack wider than 1/4 inch warrant a foundation specialist.

Roof and Water

California gets long dry stretches followed by intense rainy seasons. This pattern is brutal on roofs.

  • Roof age and remaining life. Most California roofs last 20-25 years for composition shingles, 40-50 for tile. Anything past 75% of expected life should be budgeted for replacement.
  • Signs of past roof leaks. Stained ceilings, attic water marks, peeling paint near ceiling lines. Sellers often paint over these before listing — a thermal scan can catch what paint hides.
  • Gutters and grading. Water that pools near foundations during rainy season is the leading cause of foundation movement claims in California. Check that grading slopes away from the home and gutters drain at least 6 feet from the foundation.

Fire and Wildfire Risk

If the home is in or near a designated Very High Fire Hazard Severity Zone (most of the Bay Area hills, much of Southern California canyon country, large stretches of the Sierra foothills), look for:

  • Defensible space compliance. Cleared vegetation within 30 feet of the structure is now required in fire zones. Non-compliant properties face fines and insurance issues.
  • Ember-resistant venting. Newer homes have these. Older homes often don't. Replacement is straightforward but not cheap.
  • Roof material. Wood shake roofs are essentially uninsurable in fire zones now. Composition or tile is required.
  • Prior fire damage. California sellers must disclose past fire incidents, but the standard inspection won't necessarily reveal the extent. Get a contractor estimate for any repaired fire damage you find.

Plumbing and Electrical (Age Matters)

  • Galvanized or polybutylene plumbing. Common in homes built between 1940 and 1985. Both fail predictably. Full re-plumb runs $8,000 to $20,000 depending on home size.
  • Knob-and-tube wiring. Common in homes built before 1950. Insurable in California only with significant exceptions. Full rewire: $10,000 to $25,000.
  • Aluminum branch wiring. Homes built between 1965 and 1973. Fire risk if not properly remediated.
  • Electrical panel. Federal Pacific and Zinsco panels (common in 1960s-70s California construction) have known failure modes. Replacement: $2,000 to $4,000.

Sewer and Water Supply

If the home was built before 1980, the sewer scope is not optional. California's clay soil expands and contracts with seasonal moisture. This shifts buried sewer lines and cracks old clay or cast-iron pipes. A failed sewer line replacement runs $8,000 to $30,000 depending on access and depth.

Also check the water supply line from the street to the house. Galvanized or lead service lines were standard in homes before 1960 and need replacement on most older properties.

California Home Inspection Contingency: What Your Offer Should Include

The inspection contingency is one of the most powerful tools a California buyer has. It gives you the right to walk away from the deal — keeping your earnest money deposit — if the inspection reveals issues you can't live with. It also gives you leverage to renegotiate price or request repairs.

Standard Timeline

Most California Residential Purchase Agreements (CAR Form RPA) use a default inspection contingency period of 17 days from acceptance, though shorter periods (7-10 days) are common in competitive markets like the Bay Area. The clock starts ticking the moment the seller signs the contract — not when you actually receive disclosures.

Within that window, you (or your agent) should:

  1. Schedule and complete the general home inspection (typically days 1-7)
  2. Schedule any specialist follow-up inspections (days 5-12)
  3. Review inspector findings against the seller's disclosures (days 7-14)
  4. Submit a Request for Repair or price reduction in writing, OR remove the contingency, OR cancel the contract (by day 17)

What Happens After You Find Issues

You have three meaningful options when the inspection reveals problems:

  • Request repairs from the seller. Use a Request for Repair form. The seller can accept, reject, or counter. They are not obligated to fix anything.
  • Request a credit or price reduction. Often easier than asking sellers to do the work. A $5,000 credit at closing means the seller doesn't have to find a contractor, schedule work, or pass another inspection.
  • Walk away. If the issues are too significant — or the seller refuses to negotiate — you can cancel the contract within the contingency window and recover your earnest money deposit.

The Mistake Most Buyers Make

The contingency period is short and the inspection report is long. A typical California inspection report runs 40 to 80 pages with photos. Buyers routinely focus on the headlines ("major" / "critical") and miss the moderate-severity items that add up. They also fail to cross-reference what the inspector found against what the seller disclosed — gaps between the two documents are where buyer leverage lives.

If the seller's Transfer Disclosure Statement (TDS) said "no known issues with roof" and the inspector found extensive ceiling staining, that's an unaddressed disclosure gap. Most buyers don't catch these systematically because comparing a 40-page inspection against a 12-page disclosure manually is exhausting work to do in 17 days.

Waiving the Inspection: Don't

In competitive California markets — especially the Bay Area, Los Angeles, and San Diego — buyers sometimes waive the inspection contingency to make their offer more attractive. This is one of the riskiest decisions you can make as a buyer. Without an inspection, you're relying entirely on the seller's disclosure, the agent's limited visual check, and your own untrained eye.

If you feel pressured to waive, consider a compromise: shorten the contingency period to 7-10 days instead of the standard 17, or offer to do a "pre-inspection" before making an offer (some sellers allow this). Waiving entirely should be an absolute last resort, and only for homes in clearly excellent condition.
