Persona Map · Read & Reference

Who we serve.
And how the value compounds.

OfferWise positions for eight personas across two distinct commercial motions: a self-serve SaaS funnel built around the homebuyer, and a sales-led enterprise/API motion built around real-estate-adjacent businesses that consume property-condition intelligence. The buyer is the trunk of the tree; the rest are branches that either feed buyers in or monetize their data on the way out.

How to read this page. Each persona is graded on commercial priority (Primary / Growth Multiplier / Sequenced) and motion (SaaS / Enterprise). The grading reflects what the codebase actually supports today — pricing tiers, free quotas, sales CTAs — not aspirations. Where the live thesis page diverges from current state, the divergence is flagged in-line.
Tier 1 · The Trunk
Self-Serve SaaS · Primary

The buyer is the whole business.

The buyer is paying us today. The buyer's data trains our models. The buyer's referrals reach the next buyer. Every other persona either feeds buyers into this funnel or extracts value from buyer-generated artifacts on the way through. Lose buyer-side scale and the rest of the diagram collapses.

🏠
Homebuyer
Primary Persona · 80%+ of revenue today
Primary
Job-to-be-Done
"I'm about to spend $500K to $2M. The disclosure looks clean but the inspection found problems. Should I worry, walk, or negotiate?" Buyers come into the flow at the moment of maximum financial anxiety — between offer-acceptance and inspection-contingency expiration — when speed and clarity matter more than depth.
What we sell
AI analysis of seller-disclosure + inspection-report PDFs. Output: OfferScore™ (single rating), Property Risk DNA™ (multi-dimensional risk breakdown), Seller Transparency Report™ (cross-reference of disclosure vs inspection contradictions), repair-cost breakdown with permit requirements, and a recommended offer price with negotiation talking points.
Why us, not Zillow
Zestimates price the property; they don't audit the seller's honesty. Contradiction detection between disclosure and inspection is the differentiator. No public competitor cross-references these documents. Comps + cost data are table stakes; the cross-reference engine is the moat.
Pricing: $9 / $19 / $49
Tiers: Starter · Pro · Unlimited
Acquisition: organic search, Google Ads, Reddit, inspector referral
Tier 2 · Branches that feed the trunk
Self-Serve SaaS · Growth Multipliers

Three personas exist to bring more buyers.

Inspectors, agents, and contractors aren't standalone profit centers in the current revenue mix — they're acquisition channels priced cheap or free to maximize through-flow. Their CAC for buyers is approximately zero; the buyer LTV they generate is the actual yield.

🔍
Home Inspector
Growth Multiplier · Acquisition Channel
Growth Multiplier
Job-to-be-Done
"I write a 40-page report listing 80 issues. The buyer reads three pages and panics. The agent tells them everything's fine. I lose the relationship and the next referral." Inspectors want their work understood, not misread.
What we sell
A way to give every report a buyer-friendly companion without integration work. Inspector sends the buyer a branded link. Buyer gets findings + cost estimates + permit needs in plain language. Inspector's logo on top. Free for the first 5 reports/month, then $49/mo.
Why this matters strategically
Each inspector partnership is a recurring buyer-acquisition channel. One inspector who does 5-10 reports/week is 250-500 buyers/year with near-zero CAC. The inspector loop-back email (mentioned in thesis) closes the loop by showing the inspector how their report drove offer outcomes.
Pricing: Free → $49/mo
5 reports/month free tier
Self-serve onboarding at /inspector-onboarding

Buyer's Agent
Growth Multiplier · Deal-Saver Tool
Growth Multiplier
Job-to-be-Done
"Inspection just came back. My buyer wants to lowball $50K. The list agent will walk. How do I save this deal AND protect my buyer?" Agents lose 20-30% of deals at inspection contingency; OfferWise gives them a defensible recommendation grounded in data.
What we sell
A way to look smart without becoming a structural engineer. Agent shares an OfferWise link with the buyer. Buyer gets data-backed offer recommendation. Agent positions as "I bring tools other agents don't" and earns referral revenue.
Why this matters strategically
B2B2C wedge — agent earns referral commissions, OfferWise gets the buyer relationship. Agent partnerships reduce inspection-contingency deal-fall-through, which both agents and OfferWise benefit from. White-label agent landing pages already in code (/agent-landing).

Contractor
Growth Multiplier · Marketplace Demand-Side
Sequenced
Job-to-be-Done
"Lead-gen costs me $40-100/lead from Angi/HomeAdvisor. Most are tire-kickers. I want pre-qualified buyers with budgets who already know they need work done." Contractors want intent-rich leads, not impressions.
What we sell
Pre-qualified leads for every trade identified in inspections. Buyer's report flags "you'll need a roofer + HVAC tech + electrician." Contractor pays flat $49/mo, gets matched to inspection-driven leads in their service area. No referral fees, no per-lead pricing.
Honest sequencing note
Two-sided marketplace play. Lead density requires buyer-side scale we don't have yet. Currently scaffolded but underpowered until buyer flow grows. Realistic timeline: meaningful contractor revenue is 12-24 months out, after we reach ~5K monthly buyer analyses.
Pricing: $49/mo flat
No referral fees, no commissions
Onboarding at /contractor-onboarding
Tier 3 · Branches that monetize buyer data
Sales-Led Enterprise / API · Sequenced

Four personas buy buyer-generated intelligence.

Lenders, title companies, insurance underwriters, and appraisers all consume buyer-generated property-condition data through API integrations. These are sales-led ("Talk to Us" CTAs), longer cycles, higher contract values, and entirely separate go-to-market mechanics from the SaaS funnel above. Each addresses a real underwriting or workflow gap in their industry — but each requires meaningful buyer-side data accumulation before the sale becomes credible.

🏦
Lender / Mortgage Underwriter
Enterprise · Underwriting Intelligence
Sequenced
Job-to-be-Done
"Appraisals tell me what the property is theoretically worth. They don't tell me what's actually wrong with it. I'm underwriting a 30-year loan with stale data." Inspections find condition issues appraisals miss.
What we sell
Property condition intelligence API for underwriting. Three endpoints feed loan-decision pipelines, plug into existing LOS systems, add inspection-based risk signals. Sold via "Talk to Us" — sales-led, not self-serve.
Honest sequencing note
Smaller TAM than buyer-side, higher contract value. Realistic wedge: regional/community lenders doing high-LTV loans where condition risk matters most. Big banks have internal underwriting tools they won't replace; the entry point is mid-market.
Pricing: "Talk to Us" · API tiers
Three endpoints documented at /api-docs

Insurance Underwriter
Enterprise · Pre-Bind Risk Profile
Sequenced
Job-to-be-Done
"I'm pricing a homeowners policy with whatever the broker tells me. I find out the roof is 25 years old AFTER the first claim." Property condition is the largest predictor of claims, and underwriters fly blind without it.

Output layer · The product artifacts

Three outputs cross every persona.

These are the named, trademark-protected artifacts the analysis pipeline produces. Each is consumed by buyers directly and referenced in API responses for enterprise integrations. The artifacts are how value travels from buyer-generated data into every other persona's workflow.

Single-Number Rating
OfferScore™
A 0-100 property rating summarizing condition, comp-relative price, and risk exposure. The headline number on every analysis. Designed to be defensible at a buyer's kitchen table and citable in an offer letter.
Used by: Buyers (primary), Agents (talking point), Lenders (underwriting input)
Multi-Dimensional Risk
Property Risk DNA™
A multi-axis breakdown across structural, environmental, mechanical, neighborhood, and legal dimensions. Each axis scored independently so buyers see WHERE risk concentrates, not just THAT it exists.
Used by: Buyers, Insurance Underwriters, Appraisers
Differentiator
Seller Transparency Report™
Cross-references the seller's disclosure against the inspection report and surfaces contradictions ("seller said no water damage; inspection found bathroom subfloor rot"). The single largest differentiator vs Zillow / Redfin / public comps tools.
Used by: Buyers (primary), Agents (negotiation tool)

Strategic observations · Read after the table

Six things the persona map actually says.

These observations come from reading the deployed code, not from a strategic deck. They're meant to be directional rather than definitive — to be used in product decisions, fundraising conversations, and team alignment.

Observation 01
The buyer is the entire commercial engine today.
The other seven personas exist to drive buyers into the funnel (inspectors send buyers, agents share with buyers, contractors get matched to buyers' needs) OR to monetize buyer-generated data downstream (lenders, insurance, title, appraisers buy the analysis output). The buyer is the trunk; everyone else is a branch. Every product decision should weight buyer-side outcomes 5-10x more than the others until that ratio shifts.
Observation 02
Two distinct commercial motions live in this codebase.
Personas 1-4 are self-serve SaaS with $9-$49 pricing and Stripe checkout. Personas 5-8 are sales-led enterprise/API with "Talk to Us" CTAs. These have different unit economics, different sales cycles, different team requirements. The codebase serves both but the website doesn't yet acknowledge the bifurcation visibly. Marketing surface area should be reorganized to reflect the distinction.
Observation 03
Inspector and Agent are growth multipliers, not profit centers.
Free tiers (5/month, 10/month) suggest the strategy already understands this — they're acquisition channels priced cheap or free to maximize through-flow. The CAC for buyers acquired through inspector links is essentially zero; the LTV is whatever the buyer pays. Direct inspector/agent revenue is bonus, not the goal. Maximize partnership volume; don't optimize per-partner ARR.
Observation 04
Contractor is a marketplace play that depends on buyer volume.
$49/mo flat with no per-lead fees sounds compelling — but only works at scale. With current buyer volume (~122 paid clicks per 90 days from Google Ads as of v5.87.90 GSC data), the contractor side won't have enough lead density to deliver value. Realistic activation is 12-24 months out, after buyer flow scales to ~5K monthly analyses. Until then, position contractor partnerships as "early access" rather than full GA.
Observation 05
Search intent validates buyer focus, not professional outreach.
The Search Console + Google Ads intent data (v5.87.90) shows comps queries dominate at 33.6%, tools at 12.3%, cost at 4.9% — all buyer-intent searches. Zero meaningful queries hit the for-inspectors / for-lenders surfaces. The professional personas need outbound (cold outreach, partnerships, conferences, podcast appearances) — they don't get found via search. Plan B2B GTM accordingly; don't expect SEO to solve it.
Observation 06
The mega-menu treats all 8 personas as peers. They aren't.
Buyer is 80%+ of revenue today; the others are sequenced future bets. The current top nav doesn't reflect this — Products dropdown shows seven persona links with equal visual weight. A truer hierarchy would be: Buyer (homepage) → Sub-menu of "For Professionals" with the SaaS partners → Separate "Enterprise" CTA for the API personas. The recent v5.87.91 top-bar widget work (prioritizing buyer-flow visibility over preserving all 8 persona links) was correctly aligned with this.
Recommendations · Actionable next steps

Three decisions this map should inform.

Pulled directly from the observations above. Each is a specific structural change, not a directional comment.

01
Front door = buyer. Side doors = everyone else.
The recent top-bar widget work was correct in prioritizing the buyer flow over preserving all 8 persona links in the nav. Keep going in that direction. Restructure the nav to: Buyer-led hero → "For Professionals" submenu (Inspectors, Agents, Contractors) → "Enterprise" separate CTA (Lenders, Title, Insurance, Appraisers).
02
Visually separate SaaS from Enterprise.
The 4 self-serve SaaS personas should share marketing surface area — they flow into each other and feed buyer growth. The 4 enterprise personas need a separate "Enterprise" section with sales-led CTAs, not equal nav placement. Different unit economics deserve different marketing real estate.
03
Sequence professional personas honestly.
Until buyer-side scales, professional personas are aspirational. Inspector partnership is most validated (free tier in code, lowest friction) and most strategic (recurring referral). Agent is second. Contractor needs buyer volume. Enterprise needs sales hires. Plan investment of effort accordingly; don't dilute focus across all eight equally.
